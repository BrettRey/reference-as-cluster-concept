# Carl Zimmer Review

## Overall judgment

This draft has a real argument and several usable images, but it often explains itself in philosopher-to-philosopher shorthand. A careful reader inside the subfield can follow it. A smart reader outside the subfield will often have to stop, translate, and re-read. The paper rewards attention in the octopus and communicative-calibration sections. It punishes attention in the taxonomy, HPC, and adjacent-accounts sections, where abstract terms stack up faster than concrete handles.

The octopus analogy lands. The merry-go-round mostly lands because it is simple and visual. The low-background steel analogy is vivid but too specialized to do reliable explanatory work for a general reader.

Blocking note: the missing abstract is not cosmetic. This paper especially needs a plain-English abstract because the body quickly becomes terminology-heavy.

## 2. Specific concerns

1. **Introduction**

   > "Referential grounding isn't a single threshold to cross. It's a cluster property with degree structure, where teleosemantic selection history is one stabilizer among several." (lines 37--38)

   This is the paper's core move, but it arrives in a pile of abstractions: "cluster property," "degree structure," "stabilizer." A reader outside the subfield still does not know what such a profile would look like in practice. The thesis is visible, but not yet graspable.

2. **The target argument**

   > "\term{Referential grounding} connects representations to worldly entities and properties... \term{Sensorimotor grounding} ties representations to sensory or motor states... \term{Relational grounding} locates meaning in intra-system relations... \term{Communicative grounding} is the coordination process... And \term{epistemic grounding} connects expressions to structured knowledge bases." (line 46)

   Five definitions arrive in a row, in one paragraph, with no concrete example attached to any of them. This is the kind of paragraph that makes a reader study the terminology instead of following the argument. The paper needs the taxonomy, but it currently presents it in the least reader-friendly way.

3. **The target argument**

   > "The paper's central taxonomic move is an elimination argument. Sensorimotor contact, they argue, either collapses into referential grounding... Relational grounding can't escape the representational merry-go-round... Communicative and epistemic grounding face analogous difficulties." (line 48)

   This does too much too quickly. One sentence dismisses several alternatives, but because each dismissal is compressed, the reader gets conclusion before intuition. The merry-go-round metaphor helps a little, yet the paragraph still feels like a summary of a seminar discussion the reader has not attended.

4. **Groundedness as a cluster property**

   > "The structure here is that of a \term{homeostatic property cluster} (HPC): a category defined not by necessary and sufficient conditions but by a cluster of properties held together by causal mechanisms that tend to produce co-occurrence without guaranteeing it... What makes HPC kinds scientifically useful is their \term{projectibility}..." (line 63)

   This is the hardest passage in the paper. It introduces HPC, necessary-and-sufficient conditions, causal mechanisms, co-occurrence, and projectibility in rapid succession. That is too much abstraction without a worked example. This paragraph will force many readers to slow down or give up.

5. **Groundedness as a cluster property**

   > "The dimensions of a system's \term{groundedness profile}... are causally linked... but can come apart. The profile is projectible, not merely descriptive." (line 63)

   "Projectible" is exactly the sort of word that makes non-specialists feel they have wandered into someone else's technical argument. The problem is not that the term is illegitimate. The problem is that the paper asks the reader to absorb it before showing, concretely, what prediction this framework would let us make that the binary framework would miss.

6. **Groundedness as a cluster property**

   > "Does this dissolve normativity? No.... Correctness conditions don't vanish~-- they're distributed across dimensions. A system can misrepresent along one dimension while performing well along another." (line 75)

   The claim may be philosophically important, but on the page it feels airy. "Distributed across dimensions" sounds like an answer inside the theory rather than an explanation to the reader. This is a place where one concrete case of mixed success and failure would do more work than the abstract reassurance.

7. **Communicative calibration**

   > "But it conflates the question of logical priority with the question of causal contribution." (line 82)

   This is concise philosopher's prose, not broad-audience prose. The sentence is accurate, but it asks the reader to do translation work. A reader can sense the distinction matters without immediately feeling it.

8. **Communicative calibration**

   > "The more consequential timescale is inter-generational: human interactions with one generation of models shape the training of the next..." (line 86)

   The idea is interesting, but it remains one layer too abstract. "Inter-generational" for models is slightly distancing, and the mechanism is described generically. A concrete example of what kind of correction gets preserved into a later model would make this section much more persuasive on first read.

9. **The octopus test revisited**

   > "The HPC framework explains why the intuition is so clear: with no homeostatic mechanisms operating, there's no clustering to sustain, and the system's internal states bear no projectible relation to worldly features." (line 97)

   This section begins with the paper's best image and then snaps back into its hardest vocabulary. The reader is asked to trade a vivid octopus for "homeostatic mechanisms" and "projectible relation." The argument remains understandable, but the prose loses momentum right when it should be cashing in the example.

10. **The octopus test revisited**

   > "Language produced before widespread LLM deployment is something like low-background steel manufactured before atmospheric nuclear testing..." (line 99)

   This is memorable, but it is not reliably clarifying. Low-background steel is not common knowledge, and the analogy takes a beat to decode. By the time the explanation arrives, the reader is thinking about nuclear fallout and manufacturing history rather than the paper's claim about contaminated corpora.

11. **Adjacent accounts**

   > "But 'solve versus circumvent' is a binary verdict applied to an HPC kind~-- like asking whether a borderline species is or isn't a natural kind, when the cluster properties come apart at the boundary." (line 108)

   This adds another analogy at exactly the point where the section needs simplification. Now the reader must think about species, natural kinds, and boundary cases while also keeping the LLM debate in mind. The section repeatedly asks the reader to juggle one abstraction with another.

12. **Adjacent accounts**

   > "In HPC terms, this amounts to claiming that one property in the cluster... does all the relevant work, making the others epiphenomenal." (line 112)

   "In HPC terms" is doing a lot of work throughout this section. So is "epiphenomenal." The cumulative effect is that the section reads as if it was written to prove command of the debate rather than guide a reader through it. This is the section most likely to punish even a careful non-specialist reader.

13. **Conclusion**

   > "These are questions we can investigate, and their answers will determine whether groundedness profiles are genuinely projectible..." (line 125)

   The conclusion is strongest when it is concrete and weakest when it returns to specialized vocabulary. Ending on "projectible" sends the paper back into abstraction just when it should be leaving the reader with a sharpened picture of what the framework buys.

14. **Conclusion**

   > "The metaphysical overpressure lifts." (line 127)

   This is stylish, but it is not clear. It sounds like a sentence written for rhetorical force rather than reader comprehension. A specialist may enjoy it. A general reader may pause and wonder what, exactly, has lifted.

## 3. Suggested focus for revision

- Write the abstract first, and make it aggressively plain. State the paper's move in ordinary language before the reader meets the technical vocabulary.
- In the introduction and Section 3, give the reader one concrete, recurring example of a "groundedness profile" before naming the framework around it.
- Break the taxonomy in Section 2 into smaller units and attach at least one ordinary example to the key contrasts. Right now the reader gets definitions before stakes.
- Treat `homeostatic property cluster` and `projectibility` as concepts that need immediate demonstration, not just definition. Show what they let you see.
- Keep the octopus as a running explanatory device after it is introduced. When the prose starts drifting upward, the octopus can bring it back down.
- Trim or replace analogies that require their own explanation. The low-background steel comparison is interesting but expensive.
- In the adjacent-accounts section, reduce the number of moving parts per paragraph. The current rhythm is summary, jargon, counterclaim, jargon.
- Wherever the prose says "dimension," "mechanism," "stabilizer," or "projectible," ask whether a reader has already been shown the thing, not just told its name.
