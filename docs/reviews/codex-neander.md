# Karen Neander Review

## Overall assessment

The reconstruction of Coelho Mollo and Millière is often fair, but the paper's positive proposal is not yet teleosemantically stable. The draft says that teleosemantics provides the needed normativity and that selection history distinguishes representation from mere correlation. It then redescribes selection history as one stabilizer among several and says correctness conditions are "distributed across dimensions." That is the central problem. As written, the profile framework looks less like a pluralized teleosemantics than like a multidimensional measure of reliability, coordination, and inferential competence.

My bottom line is major revision. If the paper wants to remain within a teleosemantic register, it must say much more clearly which dimensions generate genuine representational normativity, which merely improve the performance of already-grounded states, and whether selection history remains necessary for content.

## 1. Introduction — Flag

### Specific concerns

- `main.tex:35`: "training-regime selection can play the functional role of biological selection in classical teleosemantics."
  Issue: This is presented as something the reply can simply "preserve and build on." It is not a settled teleosemantic result. A selected-effects account does not automatically extend from phylogeny to gradient-based optimization. You need to distinguish at least four things here: selected effects, ontogenetic learning, design history, and derivative function. Otherwise the paper starts by taking as established the very move that most needs defense.

- `main.tex:35`: "the relevant notion of selection history extends to gradient-based optimization."
  Issue: "Extends" is too quick. The extension may be defensible, but only with an explicit account of the unit of selection, the relevant lineage, and how the historical process yields proper functions rather than merely optimized dispositions.

- `main.tex:37`: "the framework invites a binary verdict -- grounded or not."
  Issue: A conjunction of two necessary conditions does not by itself imply a crude binary. Coelho Mollo and Millière explicitly allow different causal-historical routes, limited domains, and tentative cases. If you are charging them with binarism, you need textual evidence that they are committed to a sharp threshold rather than to fallible judgment about whether the conditions are met.

### Suggested focus for revision

- Recast the opening claim about training as selection as the target paper's proposal, not as an established deliverance of classical teleosemantics.
- Show, with textual support, why the target paper should be read as committed to a binary verdict rather than to multiple, domain-sensitive grounding routes.
- Tell the reader early whether your disagreement is with the target paper's explicit view or with what you take to be a tempting but unstable way of reading it.

## 2. The target argument — Pass

### Specific concerns

- `main.tex:50`: "Selection history explains why some information-carrying states are representations and others aren't: representations were selected for their information-carrying role, and can therefore succeed or fail -- they can misrepresent."
  Issue: This is broadly right, but compressed. On a teleosemantic view, normativity does not arise from selection plus information in the abstract. It arises from proper function, which brings with it conditions under which a state is supposed to carry information and can fail to do so. The summary should be a little more careful here.

- `main.tex:52`: "The sole contention is that LLM internal states can satisfy conditions for referential grounding, making their outputs intrinsically meaningful in a specific technical sense."
  Issue: Fair enough as a summary, but it would help to note that the target paper treats some routes as more secure than others. Its post-training argument is much firmer than its claims about pre-training alone or in-context learning. Right now the reconstruction flattens those internal differences.

### Suggested focus for revision

- Add one sentence noting that the target paper distinguishes more and less secure grounding routes.
- Tighten the description of teleosemantic normativity so that "selected for carrying information" is not left floating free of proper function.

## 3. Groundedness as a cluster property — Flag

### Specific concerns

- `main.tex:57`: "the framework's implicit binarism: the two conditions, taken jointly, create a threshold that a system either crosses or doesn't."
  Issue: This remains under-argued. Having two conditions for grounding is not yet an objectionable threshold model. You need to show that the target paper cannot already accommodate degrees of epistemic confidence, limited-domain grounding, or heterogeneity in causal history.

- `main.tex:61`: "The analogy is strongest for post-training ... weaker for pre-training ... weaker still for in-context learning."
  Issue: You are sliding from graded confidence in a teleosemantic verdict to graded teleosemantic status. Those are not the same. A state either has the relevant proper function or it does not. Our evidence for that may be stronger or weaker, and the function may attach in some subsystems and not others, but that is not yet a scalar property called "teleosemantic selection."

- `main.tex:63`: "The profile is projectible, not merely descriptive."
  Issue: This is asserted, not shown. An HPC category needs real homeostatic mechanisms that explain co-occurrence and support prediction. The section mostly lists desiderata or contributors to good performance. It does not yet show that these properties form a homeostatic cluster rather than an analyst's convenience class.

- `main.tex:65`: "teleosemantic selection: the degree to which training or developmental history has selected for states that carry information about the world and function to track worldly features."
  Issue: Proper functions are not introduced here with enough discipline. What is varying by degree: the strength of selection, the breadth of domains over which a function applies, the confidence with which we can attribute a function, or the amount of downstream reliability? Those are importantly different.

- `main.tex:67`: "causal-informational coupling: the directness, robustness, and fidelity of causal chains linking internal states to worldly entities."
  Issue: Causal-informational linkage by itself does not underwrite misrepresentation. Correlation can fail, but that is not yet representational failure. Without proper function, consumer structure, or some other source of correctness conditions, this looks like a causal precondition for content, not an independent normative dimension.

- `main.tex:69`: "communicative calibration: the degree to which a system's representations have been shaped by interactive feedback anchoring usage to shared worldly reference."
  Issue: Again, this may improve coordination or learning, but the section does not show that it independently makes a state the kind of thing that can misrepresent. A community can repair usage around an already meaningful term; that does not show that repair constitutes basic representational content.

- `main.tex:71`: "inferential-role structure: the degree to which internal organisation supports correct inferential transitions about worldly domains."
  Issue: This imports the inferentialist pressure that the section officially resists. Good inferential organization can support reasoning with already meaningful states. It does not by itself supply aboutness. The paper says as much elsewhere, but then counts inferential role as a contributor to groundedness without saying what kind of normativity it contributes.

- `main.tex:75`: "Correctness conditions don't vanish -- they're distributed across dimensions. A system can misrepresent along one dimension while performing well along another. The normativity is real but pluralistic."
  Issue: This is the most serious problem in the paper. Misrepresentation is not obviously something that comes "in dimensions" here. A state can fail to indicate what it is supposed to indicate. But what is it for a state to "misrepresent along the communicative-calibration dimension"? Or along the inferential-role dimension? Those sound like failures of coordination or reasoning, not failures of representation. Unless each dimension independently supports genuine world-directed correctness conditions, teleosemantic normativity has leaked away.

- `main.tex:77`: "Novel contents might be stabilised primarily through communicative calibration and inferential-role structure rather than through selection history narrowly conceived."
  Issue: This is not a preservation of teleosemantics. It is a retreat from it, unless you rework the discussion in terms of expanded notions of proper function. Roloff's complaint is not that selection history should become optional. It is that selected-effects teleosemantics needs a richer account of proper function, including derived, relational, or adapted functions. As written, the paper uses the novel-content objection to license non-teleosemantic substitutes.

### Suggested focus for revision

- Decide whether teleosemantic selection is necessary for representational content. If it is, the other dimensions should be presented as modulators of robustness, accessibility, or performance of already-grounded representations. If it is not, stop saying that teleosemantics is preserved.
- Distinguish sharply between graded evidence for attributing content and graded content itself.
- For each proposed dimension, state whether it independently supports world-directed misrepresentation. If not, say so and downgrade its theoretical role.
- Either provide a real HPC argument, with identified homeostatic mechanisms and predictive payoffs, or remove the Boyd framing and describe the view more modestly as a diagnostic decomposition.

## 4. Communicative calibration — Flag

### Specific concerns

- `main.tex:82`: "it conflates the question of logical priority with the question of causal contribution."
  Issue: Possibly, but the section never establishes the stronger claim you need. Teleosemantically, a factor may causally contribute to successful reference without being a source of representational normativity. The target paper can happily admit causal contribution from communication while still insisting that communicative grounding is downstream of referential grounding in the explanatory order that matters here.

- `main.tex:84`: "Reference ... isn't established once and then communicated. It's stabilised through the communicative process."
  Issue: Clark's account concerns the achievement of successful definite reference by interlocutors who already possess representational capacities and shared practices. That is a pragmatic story about coordinating reference, not obviously a naturalistic story about how a vehicle first acquires content. You need an argument bridging that gap.

- `main.tex:86`: "Across successive models, the cumulative effect of millions of corrective exchanges pushes the population of models toward more reliable worldly reference."
  Issue: If this is meant teleosemantically, then the normative work is still being done by selection across training histories. In that case communicative calibration is not an independent dimension; it is one source of selected proper functions. If it is not meant teleosemantically, then the section is again trading in reliability and performance without showing how correctness conditions arise.

- `main.tex:86`: "The mechanism isn't identical to face-to-face conversational repair, but the functional structure is analogous."
  Issue: Analogous in what respect that matters for content? Similarity in feedback architecture is not enough. Teleosemantics cares about the historically selected role of states, not just the existence of error-corrective loops.

- `main.tex:88`: "LLMs inherit a version of this structure."
  Issue: This is too fast. Putnam's division of linguistic labour presupposes speakers embedded in practices of deference, uptake, correction, and shared referential norms. Statistical exposure to the products of those practices is not obviously participation in them. At minimum, the section needs to address the danger that the model's content is merely derivative on human users' representational capacities.

### Suggested focus for revision

- Separate communicative calibration as evidence of existing content from communicative calibration as a possible historical route by which selected functions are acquired.
- Clarify the unit of selection if the retraining-across-generations story is supposed to do teleosemantic work.
- Address directly whether the content you are attributing here is intrinsic or merely derivative on human linguistic practices.

## 5. The octopus test revisited — Flag

### Specific concerns

- `main.tex:97`: "the octopus scores near zero on every dimension."
  Issue: This is an artifact of your framework, not an independent argument. The section uses the profile framework to redescribe the case and then treats that redescription as explanatory support for the framework.

- `main.tex:97`: "change the stabiliser regime and the intuition shifts."
  Issue: Of course intuitions shift if one changes the case. But then one is no longer evaluating the octopus case as posed. You need to show that Bender and Koller's lesson depends on illicitly holding fixed too much, not merely that nearby cases differ.

- `main.tex:99`: "systems that reshape the linguistic environment they inhabit aren't merely spinning the representational merry-go-round."
  Issue: Causal impact on the environment is not yet representational grounding. Thermostats, parasites, and bureaucracies reshape environments too. The relevant question is whether the internal states have proper functions to carry information about worldly conditions and can therefore misrepresent. "World impact" is too weak.

- `main.tex:99`: "This reciprocal shaping ... is evidence of causal coupling."
  Issue: Even if true, causal coupling is not enough. The section again risks replacing representational normativity with a looser notion of causal embeddedness.

### Suggested focus for revision

- Keep the reply to the octopus test tied to the specific teleosemantic machinery: selection history, proper function, and the conditions under which outputs count as correct or incorrect.
- Remove or sharply constrain claims that mere causal impact on a linguistic environment is evidence of grounding.
- Make clearer whether the point is that the octopus case is unlike contemporary LLMs in relevant historical respects, or that the octopus intuition itself is unstable.

## 6. Adjacent accounts — Flag

### Specific concerns

- `main.tex:108`: "solve versus circumvent is a binary verdict applied to an HPC kind."
  Issue: This is too concessive to your own framework and too dismissive of the opposing view. Until you have shown that grounding is an HPC kind, you cannot use that claim to dispose of an opponent's binary distinction.

- `main.tex:110`: "the question is how much and what kind of grounding, not whether grounding is categorically present or absent."
  Issue: This may be right at the level of our epistemic uncertainty or at the level of robustness. It is not yet clearly right at the level of representational status. Again: are you grading evidence, robustness, or content?

- `main.tex:112`: "inferential role without world-linking stabilisers remains trapped in the representational merry-go-round."
  Issue: Fair as criticism of inferentialism. But the paper has not yet shown that its own "world-linking stabilisers" other than teleosemantic selection actually link representation to the world in the normatively relevant sense.

- `main.tex:114`: "Structural correspondence that explains performance is evidence of causal-informational coupling and inferential-role structure working together."
  Issue: Evidence for structural correspondence or successful task performance is not automatically evidence for representational content. This paragraph drifts toward an explanatory-success criterion that bypasses the teleosemantic burden you took on earlier.

- `main.tex:116`: "increasing stabilisation through deployment feedback and world-interaction is empirically tractable, not categorically foreclosed."
  Issue: That may be true, but it is compatible with teleosemantic skepticism about current systems. The section repeatedly assimilates adjacent views to your framework without squarely stating where the framework actually departs from them.

### Suggested focus for revision

- Use this section to sharpen disagreements, not to redescribe every opponent as already speaking your language.
- Stop relying on the HPC framing as a premise in disputes with other views until the framing itself has been defended.
- Reconnect each comparison to the normativity question: what, on your view, makes a state capable of misrepresentation?

## 7. Conclusion — Flag

### Specific concerns

- `main.tex:121`: "correctness conditions require selection history, and selection history is what distinguishes representations from mere correlations."
  Issue: If this is true, then selection history cannot also be merely one stabilizer among several coequal dimensions of groundedness. This sentence states the teleosemantic line correctly, but it conflicts with the paper's positive proposal.

- `main.tex:123`: "Referential grounding is a cluster property stabilised by heterogeneous mechanisms: teleosemantic selection, causal-informational coupling, communicative calibration, and inferential-role structure."
  Issue: This is the unresolved tension in one sentence. Are the last three contributors to representational content, or contributors to the reliability and usability of already representational states? The paper wants them to do content-level work, but does not show how.

- `main.tex:125`: "Does in-context learning contribute genuine selection pressure or only statistical pattern-matching?"
  Issue: This is a good question, but in the present draft it is treated too casually. If in-context learning is to matter teleosemantically, the paper must say whether the relevant proper function belongs to transient activations, to the pre-trained mechanism enabling the adaptation, or to both. The target paper is careful here; your conclusion should be too.

- `main.tex:127`: "We don't need a single decisive criterion to determine whether vector representations mean anything."
  Issue: This risks sounding like an abandonment of the content question rather than a refinement of it. Teleosemantics is not looking for a grab-bag of favorable indicators. It is trying to say what makes a state representational at all.

### Suggested focus for revision

- State one clean relationship between the profile framework and teleosemantic content attribution.
- Either say: selection history remains necessary, while the other dimensions measure robustness of already-grounded content; or say: the paper is replacing teleosemantics with a looser pluralist framework. At present it tries to say both.
- Tighten the conclusion around the locus of proper function, especially for pre-training, post-training, and in-context learning.

## What most needs fixing

- The paper must stop sliding between three different claims: that selection history is necessary for content, that selection history is only one contributor among several, and that non-selection dimensions can themselves support correctness conditions.
- The paper must distinguish representational normativity from epistemic reliability, communicative success, and inferential organization.
- The paper must either defend the HPC apparatus properly or demote it from ontology to heuristic.
- The paper must be clearer about whether it is revising teleosemantics from within or leaving it behind while retaining some of its vocabulary.
