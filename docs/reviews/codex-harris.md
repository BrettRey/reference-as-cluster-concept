# Randy Harris Review

## Overall assessment

This is a serious and mostly fair reply, and it does honor Rapoport's Rules in part. You restate the target clearly, you register genuine agreement, and you avoid the cheap move of dismissing the target as confused. Where the paper falls short is the third Rapoport move: I don't yet get a crisp account of what the target taught you that your own framework could not have seen without it. Because that learning step stays implicit, the later claim that the target "invites a binary verdict" starts to look less like charitable development and more like a rhetorically useful simplification.

The central move is clear enough in slogan form: replace a threshold question with a profile question. The problem is that the paper bundles three different claims and moves as if they stand or fall together: groundedness is graded, groundedness is multi-dimensional, and groundedness is an HPC kind with projectible causal structure. The first claim is plausible. The second is interesting. The third is the ambitious one, and the paper currently announces it faster than it argues it.

For a PhiMiSci readership, the prose is generally lucid at the sentence level, but the middle sections accumulate technical nouns too quickly: teleosemantics, causal-informational relations, selection history, HPC, projectibility, stabilisers, inferential-role structure. This audience can handle the vocabulary. What they won't forgive is concept stacking without enough local payoff.

## 2. Specific concerns

### Introduction

- "`But the framework invites a binary verdict -- grounded or not -- that obscures what's actually at stake.`"  
  This is the hinge of the whole paper, and it arrives before you've shown that Coelho Mollo and Milliere really force that reading. Until that burden is discharged, sympathetic readers will suspect that the reply depends on flattening the target into a threshold view.

- "`This is a productive contribution, and it's worth being explicit about what it gets right.`"  
  Good move. But the section never quite completes the Rapoport sequence by saying what you learned from them that changed your own view. Agreement is present; indebtedness is still mostly ceremonial.

- "`Communicative calibration, inferential-role structure, and causal-informational coupling each contribute to a system's groundedness profile`"  
  Too many abstractions land before the reader has one concrete case in hand. The central move is intelligible, but not yet palpable.

### The target argument

- "`The paper's central taxonomic move is an elimination argument.`"  
  This is a useful condensation of the target, and the section is largely successful. My only caution is that because this reconstruction is so clean, the later charge of "implicit binarism" needs textual proof of the same quality. Otherwise the paper feels more charitable in reconstruction than in critique.

### Groundedness as a cluster property

- "`The structure here is that of a homeostatic property cluster (HPC)`"  
  This is the paper's real argumentative leap, and it happens too fast. A reader can grant gradedness and even multiple dimensions without granting HPC metaphysics, homeostatic mechanisms, or projectibility. You need to argue for the extra machinery, not just name it.

- "`The profile is projectible, not merely descriptive.`"  
  This is asserted as a payoff before it is shown as a result. Where is the worked example in which knowing a system's profile lets us predict a further property better than rival descriptions would?

- "`At least four stabilising mechanisms are visible in the existing literature.`"  
  The paper slides among `conditions`, `dimensions`, `mechanisms`, and `stabilisers` as if they were interchangeable. They are not. This blurs the architecture of the proposal at exactly the point where the reader needs sharp distinctions.

- "`Models fine-tuned against factuality norms score higher on this dimension than base models trained purely on next-token prediction.`"  
  That may be true, but here it is doing argumentative work without visible empirical support. The paper wants philosophical traction from empirical differences; then it needs at least one anchored case, not just plausible gestures.

- "`Correctness conditions don't vanish -- they're distributed across dimensions.`"  
  Elegant sentence, but too quick. Distribution can sound like dilution. Which dimensions generate bona fide correctness conditions, and which merely improve reliability? Until you answer that, the teleosemantic audience will hear a leak in the normativity.

- "`The profile records this rather than forcing a binary verdict.`"  
  This repeated contrast is beginning to do the work of an opponent characterization. The more often it recurs, the more the paper must prove that the opponent is genuinely committed to binary architecture.

### Communicative calibration

- "`But it conflates the question of logical priority with the question of causal contribution.`"  
  This is a strong sentence and probably the section's best move. The problem is that the rest of the section does not yet show causal contribution with enough specificity.

- "`Across successive models, the cumulative effect of millions of corrective exchanges pushes the population of models toward more reliable worldly reference.`"  
  This is a large empirical claim presented as though it were common ground. Who curates those exchanges? In which domains does the correction improve reference rather than mere compliance? The rhetoric outruns the warrant.

- "`The mechanism isn't identical to face-to-face conversational repair, but the functional structure is analogous`"  
  The analogy is carrying too much weight. Changing from interlocutors in a shared episode to distributed human feedback across model generations is not just a scale change; it is a mechanism change.

- "`LLMs inherit a version of this structure.`"  
  Putnam's division of linguistic labour depends on deference, role differentiation, and communal norm uptake. An LLM does not defer. The analogy is stimulating, but as written it looks borrowed rather than earned.

### The octopus test revisited

- "`In profile terms, the octopus scores near zero on every dimension.`"  
  This is question-begging unless you first show that your dimensions illuminate something the octopus thought experiment was missing. Right now it can read as if the framework wins because it defines the opponent case as deficient.

- "`Suppose the octopus were trained on messages from thousands of speakers with feedback correcting its errors.`"  
  At that point you are no longer answering the octopus test; you are replacing it with a different case. That can be legitimate, but then say so explicitly and explain why the modified case is dialectically relevant to Bender and Koller.

- "`LLMs now act in the world.`"  
  True in a broad sociotechnical sense, but the paper's target is representational content. Here the argument risks sliding from semantic grounding to worldly effects. Readers in philosophy of mind will notice the category shift immediately.

- "`Language produced before widespread LLM deployment is something like low-background steel manufactured before atmospheric nuclear testing`"  
  Striking analogy, but it is rhetorically too loud for the work it does. It attracts attention to itself rather than clarifying the semantic point.

- "`But it's evidence of causal coupling`"  
  Not obviously. Causing downstream linguistic effects is not yet the same as being referentially anchored to worldly entities. This is the place where critics will suspect equivocation on "coupling."

### Adjacent accounts

- "`The profile framework sits between positions in the current debate. Five recent accounts illustrate the space.`"  
  Sensible section plan. The problem is execution: the section reads as if every rival view mainly exists to be absorbed into your framework. That weakens the paper's ethos, because it stops looking like engagement and starts looking like annexation.

- "`solve versus circumvent is a binary verdict applied to an HPC kind`"  
  This sentence depends on the very HPC framing still under dispute. It therefore cannot do decisive work against Floridi et al. until the HPC case has been independently secured.

- "`In HPC terms, this amounts to claiming that one property in the cluster -- inferential-role structure -- does all the relevant work`"  
  This is rhetorically efficient, but not fully fair. It translates the inferentialist view into your vocabulary before showing that inferentialists should accept that translation.

- "`The profile framework agrees on both counts`"  
  This recurring move appears in several paragraphs in this section. Too often, the paper's response to adjacent views is "my framework can absorb that." Readers will want at least one place where an objection genuinely bites and forces modification or sharper limits.

### Conclusion

- "`The disagreement here is about the architecture of the answer, not its foundations.`"  
  Attractive line, but it softens the scale of the disagreement too much. If teleosemantic selection is no longer doing the singular normative work, that is not merely architectural trimming.

- "`These are questions we can investigate`"  
  Good turn toward empirical tractability. But the paper has not yet shown how the profile dimensions would be operationalized or measured, so the promise currently feels programmatic.

- "`The groundedness profile is that framework.`"  
  This closes as though the case were settled. The paper has made a promising proposal; it has not yet established that this is the framework.

## 3. Suggested focus for revision

- Make the Rapoport move explicit. Add one clear sentence, early, stating what the target paper taught you and what narrower disagreement remains once that lesson is absorbed.

- Separate the three claims now bundled together: `graded`, `multi-dimensional`, and `HPC/projectible`. If you cannot fully defend all three in this paper, narrow the ambition and defend the strongest one well.

- Tighten the architecture of the central section. Decide whether teleosemantic selection, causal coupling, communicative calibration, and inferential-role structure are dimensions, mechanisms, or evidential indicators, and keep those roles stable.

- Rework communicative calibration so it shows a specific route from interaction to reference, not just a family resemblance between dialogue and retraining.

- Rebuild the octopus section on stricter dialectical terms. First answer what the original thought experiment is meant to show; only then introduce regime changes and explain why they matter.

- In the adjacent-accounts section, let one or two rival objections remain sharp instead of immediately translating them into your own framework. The paper will sound more trustworthy if it is seen absorbing pressure, not just redistributing it.

- Reduce concept stacking by carrying one concrete example across sections. At the moment, the paper often introduces a new abstraction where a recurring case could do the rhetorical work more cleanly.
